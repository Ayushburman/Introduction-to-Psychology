Learning psychology is a great goal, but it's important to start with a realistic expectation:

> **No one can literally "read minds."** Psychology helps you make better-informed hypotheses about thoughts, emotions, and behavior—not know them with certainty.

If your goal is to understand strangers more accurately, the best approach combines psychology, observation, communication, and critical thinking.

# Phase 1: Build the Foundation (2–4 weeks)

Start with these core areas:

1. Cognitive Psychology

   * Attention
   * Memory
   * Decision-making
   * Perception

2. Social Psychology

   * First impressions
   * Group behavior
   * Social influence
   * Attribution errors

3. Personality Psychology

   * Trait theories
   * Individual differences
   * The Big Five model

4. Behavioral Psychology

   * Reinforcement
   * Habits
   * Conditioning

---

# Phase 2: Learn to Observe Instead of Assume

When meeting someone, notice observable facts before drawing conclusions.

Observe:

* Posture
* Walking pace
* Facial expressions
* Eye contact
* Voice volume
* Speaking speed
* Hand movements
* Clothing (without stereotyping)
* Personal space
* Turn-taking in conversation

Instead of thinking:

❌ "They're lying."

Think:

✅ "They avoided eye contact when discussing this topic. There are many possible explanations, so I'll look for more evidence."

---

# Phase 3: Understand Emotions

Learn to recognize common emotional expressions such as:

* Happiness
* Sadness
* Anger
* Fear
* Surprise
* Disgust

Remember that expressions vary across people and cultures, and no single facial movement reliably reveals deception.

---

# Phase 4: Learn Active Listening

Good "people reading" depends more on listening than guessing.

Pay attention to:

* Word choice
* Repeated themes
* Tone of voice
* Hesitations
* Confidence level
* Consistency over time

Useful questions include:

* "What happened next?"
* "How did that affect you?"
* "Can you tell me more about that?"

---

# Phase 5: Learn Personality Models

One of the most researched models is the **Big Five**:

* Openness
* Conscientiousness
* Extraversion
* Agreeableness
* Neuroticism

Rather than placing people into rigid "types," think of these as dimensions that vary from person to person.
